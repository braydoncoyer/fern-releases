Added

  • PDF previews in your notes. Drop or paste a PDF and Atlas shows the document right in the note — a full-width card with the first page, a subtle border, and a page count — instead of a bare file link. On Mac you can scroll the whole PDF in place without leaving the note; on iPad and iPhone, tap the preview to read it full-screen with zoom, search, and share. Move the caret into the line and the card steps aside so you can edit the raw link, just like other Markdown. Your file stays a standard Markdown link, so notes still open cleanly anywhere else. Put the link on its own line for a preview; a link mid-sentence stays a link.
  • Nearby Notes (Mac & iPad). While you write, Atlas quietly looks for notes related to the one that's open — ones you haven't linked yet. When it finds something, a soft glow rises from the bottom of the editor: no popups, nothing to dismiss. Move your pointer toward it (Mac) or tap the pill (iPad) and a row of suggestion cards appears, each explaining why that note belongs nearby, with High/Medium/Low confidence and a Tension badge when a note pushes back on what you're writing. Dismiss a suggestion and it stays gone.
  • Tap a suggestion card for the full story (Mac & iPad). A card expands App Store-style into a centered panel — the card itself visibly grows into place — with a deeper read on how the two notes connect. From there, Link it (or Draft thought, when the connection wants a short bridging line) stages a reviewable draft in your note, Ignore retires the suggestion for good, and Esc or a tap outside shrinks the card back into its row.
  • Connection details on iPhone. In the ✦ Connections sheet, tap a suggestion to open its own page: the full explanation plus the deeper read, with Link it / Draft thought and Ignore actions. Linking closes the sheet and lands you on the staged draft, ready to review.
  • Readable daily-note dates. Daily notes now display as "June 30, 2026" instead of "2026-06-30" everywhere Atlas shows their titles — the note's own heading in the editor, the All Notes cards and Today row, the sidebar, Version History, and the Home Screen widgets. It's display-only: your Markdown files keep their YYYY-MM-DD names and headings (tap into the title and the raw date shows, like any other Markdown syntax). Prefer the raw dates everywhere? Turn off Settings → Daily Notes → Readable dates.
  • Home Screen widgets (iPhone & iPad). Add Atlas to your Home Screen to keep your notes close. The Daily Note widget shows today's date, a preview of what you've written (or a nudge to start), and your word count — tap it to jump straight into today's note (small and medium sizes). The Recent Notes widget lists the notes you've opened most recently and taps straight back into any of them (medium and large sizes). Both match Atlas's look in light and dark mode.

Changed

  • A friendlier first run, on every device. Onboarding was redesigned around getting you writing in under a minute. iPad now gets a composed setup card instead of a sparse full-screen column, and iPhone gets its own thumb-first flow — a full-width Continue button pinned to the bottom, text that follows your Dynamic Type size, and touch-language tips. The Mac's four-step welcome stays, with tighter copy and a fresh opening line. The sidebar — and on Mac, the context panel — now open by default, and on Mac the panel stays put as you move between notes; ⌘\ and ⌘⌥I tuck them away when you want nothing but the words (the welcome note shows how).
  • A starter vault that speaks your language. The notes seeded into a new vault were rewritten to be shorter and more direct, and they're now written for the device you start on — keyboard shortcuts on Mac, touch gestures on iPhone and iPad. The welcome note is half its old length; the deep dives live in companion notes. The out-of-left-field sleep/focus examples are gone: in their place, two short notes about writing and ideas that circle the same themes without linking each other — open one and watch Discovered Connections find the other. Both carry an #ideas tag, and a new Tags & Regions guide explains Atlas's flat structure — no folders, with All Notes as the everything view — then walks you through turning that tag into your first Region (the Trails walkthrough note retired; you'll meet trails when you ask Atlas a question). The Privacy & AI note now includes a five-minute setup walkthrough with direct links to the Voyage AI and OpenAI key dashboards, for anyone who skipped keys during onboarding.
  • A new app icon. Atlas's glass drafting-compass "A" replaces the old mark everywhere — Dock, Home Screen, and the welcome flow.
  • Connections read your notes paragraph by paragraph. Atlas upgraded to Voyage's context-aware embeddings with paragraph-scale chunks, so search, Ask, and discovered connections match on the specific passage that relates — not just the note's overall topic. Your library re-indexes itself once after updating (this uses your Voyage key in the background; writing is unaffected while it runs).
  • Connection explanations describe the relationship. The "why connected" text now tells you how the two notes relate — quoting the phrases they share — instead of suggesting an edit. What actually happens when you link is the button's job: Link it weaves existing words into a link; Draft thought writes the short bridge for you to review.
  • Reviewing an AI draft on iPhone. Accept/Reject now appears as its own glass bar across the bottom of the screen — thumb-reachable, above the keyboard, and the floating buttons step aside until you've decided — instead of a small pill that could hide behind them.
  • The Format panel comes to iPad. Tap Aa in the shortcuts bar above the keyboard and the same styled panel the iPhone gets opens as a popover — text styles named the way you read them (Title, Heading, Subheading, Body, Quote) with the active style highlighted live, pill-shaped control groups, and the link actions. It replaces the old flat "Turn Into" menu; the quick bold/italic/strikethrough/highlight/code taps stay on the bar. With a hardware keyboard the floating strip slims to just Aa and Link (the ⌘-shortcuts cover the inline marks) and the panel opens beside your cursor instead of over the keys.
  • The Format sheet now floats like a card (iPhone). Tapping Aa presents formatting as a rounded card with a small gap all the way around — its bottom corners follow the curve of the screen — instead of an edge-to-edge panel on a gray backdrop. Text styles read the way you'd say them (Title, Heading, Subheading, Body, Quote), each shown in its own type, with pill-shaped button groups.

Fixed

  • Your text holds still when panels open (Mac). Opening or closing the sidebar or the right panel could make lines of text flicker and briefly rewrap onto the next line. The writing column now keeps its exact width while panels slide in and out, so the note never shifts under you.
  • The editing buttons are proper Liquid Glass (iPhone). Undo and Format ("Aa") no longer paint an opaque white fill inside their toolbar pill — the glass shows through as intended, in light and dark mode.
  • The sidebar's Atlas menu no longer collides with long lists (iPad & iPhone). The bottom "Atlas" row now sits on a bar material, so a long Recent section blurs beneath it instead of showing through the label.
  • Lists line up the way you'd expect (Mac, iPad & iPhone). Bullets, numbered items, and checkboxes now share the same text column; nested items step in evenly; single- and double-digit numbers right-align so their periods stack; and the task checkbox sits snug against its text instead of floating in dead space. Wrapped lines hang exactly under the item's text everywhere.
  • Quotes keep one continuous accent bar (Mac, iPad & iPhone). A quote that wraps onto more lines — or runs across several lines — now draws a single unbroken bar down its left edge, instead of a broken dash on every line.
  • Version History always shows the version you tapped (iPad). Rapidly tapping through the timeline could briefly paint the previous version's preview or diff; each tap now lands on the right one.
  • Version History opens each version in Rendered view (Mac). The Rendered｜Changes toggle now resets when you pick a different version, matching iPad and iPhone, instead of staying stuck on Changes.
  • Swiping Restore on an unchanged version no longer clutters the timeline (iPhone). Restoring a version identical to the note's current content is now a no-op instead of recording a pointless restore point.
  • Pasting a link over selected text undoes in one step (Mac). Wrapping a selection as a markdown link by pasting a URL is now a single named undo action, matching iPad and iPhone.
